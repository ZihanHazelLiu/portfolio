PhD Student in Informatics, University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign

I study how conversational AI shapes human meaning-making in interpretive domains — contexts where meaning is subjective and without a single correct answer, like humor, social norms, and emotional uncertainty — and what this implies for how people think, judge, and communicate with and through AI.

About

A few things about me

I came to this PhD wanting to understand the work of interpretation itself — the way people make sense of confusing emotional moments, the subconscious processes behind judgment, the way meaning is found rather than given. My research grew out of that curiosity. I study conversational AI not primarily as a productivity tool, but as a force that shapes how people think when there isn't a single right answer.

My work moves across human-computer interaction, computational social science, and AI in education. I'm currently looking for research internship opportunities for Summer 2026.

Before UIUC, I worked with Prof. Yi-Chieh Lee at the National University of Singapore on cross-cultural studies of conversational agents, and earned my B.S. in Software Engineering from Sichuan University. I share my apartment with two cats, 小黑 and 小奶牛.

Projects

01

The Social Gaze of LLMs: A Systematic Review of Multimodal Approaches to Human Behavior Understanding

First author · 2025 · arXiv preprint, under review

LLM-powered multimodal systems are increasingly used to interpret human social behavior — in classrooms, clinics, robots, and surveillance settings — yet there has been no systematic account of how researchers actually apply these models' "social competence." I led a systematic review of 176 publications across HCI, AI, healthcare, and education, developing a four-dimensional coding framework (application, technical, evaluative, ethical) and a human-in-the-loop, LLM-assisted coding pipeline.

The review surfaces four interlocking patterns: a heavy concentration on social perception and reasoning with little work on interaction or creativity; a "modality-to-text bottleneck" that compresses rich audiovisual signals into transcripts before reasoning; evaluation practices that privilege static benchmarks over socially grounded human-centered assessment; and ethical engagement that addresses privacy concretely while leaving fairness and societal harms largely unmitigated. From these patterns I argue that social creativity — the capacity to generate multiple plausible interpretations of an ambiguous social situation — is a research opportunity that current convergent design paradigms systematically underdevelop.

02

Understanding Public Perceptions of AI Conversational Agents: A Cross-Cultural Analysis

First author · CHI 2024 · with Y.-C. Lee (NUS), R. Zhang, A. Chen, H. Li

This was the first large-scale computational study of how culture shapes public perceptions of conversational agents. Working with approximately one million social media posts from Twitter (U.S.) and Sina Weibo (China) collected over a five-year window, my collaborators and I combined BERTopic for thematic analysis with S-WEAT — a word-embedding adaptation of the Word Embedding Association Test — to measure perceptions of CAs along three dimensions: warmth, competence, and emotional valence.

Several findings reframe how the field thinks about CA design. Chinese users approach CAs hedonically — repurposing utilitarian assistants like Siri for socio-emotional play — and perceive voice-based and physically embodied CAs as warmer and more competent. U.S. users approach them functionally with an ambivalent emotional tone. Across both countries, warmth perception is the strongest predictor of positive emotional valence, outweighing competence. We argue for a "warmth primacy" approach to CA design and a context-conscious stance: a feature that helps in one cultural setting can backfire in another.

03

AI-Enhanced Speech-Language Intervention Documentation: Opportunities and Design Goals

Co-author · AIED 2025 · with Q. Zheng, A. Choudhry, Y. Huang, J. Xiong

Speech-language pathologists face crushing documentation workloads — SOAP notes for every session, often written from memory after the fact — that contribute directly to burnout and reduced time for actual care. This project is a formative human-centered study of how vision-language models could support that work. We conducted think-aloud sessions with 17 SLPs, having them review AI-generated session analyses produced by GPT-4V and iteratively refine the prompts that produced them.

My contribution centered on translating the team's qualitative findings into a structured Prompt Sheet — a tool that helps AI engineers embed task-specific, developmental, and individualized context into LLM-driven documentation. The Sheet operationalizes three fluidity requirements that current systems systematically fail: contextual fluidity (distinguishing structured practice from spontaneous skill use), assessment fluidity (knowing when not to flag an error outside the current learning focus), and individual fluidity (recognizing non-standard behaviors as engagement rather than disengagement). The deeper finding: accurate AI-assisted behavior interpretation requires embedding the kind of nuanced, person-specific knowledge that clinicians carry — knowledge that resists easy formalization.
